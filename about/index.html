About

My name is Emily Lindemer, and this site in a collection of my work, both ongoing and completed projects, and my general interests.

My educational background straddles both computer science and neuroscience, with a strong emphasis in research. I completed my Bachelor's of Science from McGill University in Montreal with a major in computational neuroscience and a minor in computer science. During my time at McGill, I conducted research in three different laboratories whose focuses ranged from single-cell tracings in macaque cortices, understanding the human visual system's ability to perceive motion, and investigation of human motor control for both speech and limb motion. My undergraduate thesis research was published in the McGill Student Undergraduate Research Journal. I also spent a year as a teaching assistant for Introduction to Computing which focused on the fundamentals of Java programming.

After completing my undergraduate degree, I moved to Boston, MA to work as a research assistant at the Boston VA Healthcare System in a neuroimaging laboratory focusing on both the physical and functional affects of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and traumatic brain injury (TBI) on the human brain in a popuation of combat veterans. During this time I learned about medical imaging -- both acquisition and post-processing -- and deepened my understanding of statistical methods for scientific research. My two years at the VA culminated in a scientific publication in which I describe a novel metric for the assessment of the cumulative lifetime burden of PTSD on the human brain and its relationship to brain structure.

Following my work at the VA, I enrolled in the Division of Health Sciences and Technology PhD program in Medical Engineering and Medical Physics. This program focuses on training medical engineers to actually speak the language of clinicians to ensure the development of highly translational healthcare-related technologies. It involves a significant amount of coursework at Harvard Medical School and a total of three months of full-time clinical work on an inpatient internal medicine ward. It also includes the full PhD coursework of a selected MIT program which I chose to be computer science for my degree. In keeping with my interest for neuroimaging, I joined the Laboratory for Computational Neuroimaging at the Athinoula A. Martinos Center for Biomedical Imaging at Massachusetts General Hospital. One of the primary focuses in this lab is the development of the open source neuroimaging processing software called FreeSurfer, towards which I contributed development work at various times throught my tenure in the lab. I also regularly taught lectures in statistics as part of the FreeSurfer course both locally and at international course offerings. In my graduate research, I primarily worked with human brain MRI data from older individuals both with and without Alzheimer's disease (AD). I focused on assessing and investigating subtle changes to the brain's white matter that occur in both aging and age-related disease processes such as AD. This research involved both the development of machine-learning based image processing tools as well as the application of these tools to answer clinical questions. These tools are hosted on my personal github site and the publications resulting from my work are listed in my CV. My final PhD dissertation can be found here.
